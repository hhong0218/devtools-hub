Cron Expression Examples

Cron is the time-based scheduler behind countless automated jobs — backups, report emails, cache warmers, and cleanup scripts. Its syntax is terse, and it's easy to forget which field is which. This guide breaks down the five fields and gives you a copy-ready table of the most common schedules in plain English.

The five cron fields

A standard cron expression has five space-separated fields:

┌── minute (0-59)
│ ┌── hour (0-23)
│ │ ┌── day of month (1-31)
│ │ │ ┌── month (1-12)
│ │ │ │ ┌── day of week (0-6, Sunday = 0)
│ │ │ │ │
* * * * *

Each field accepts a few special characters: * means "every value", , lists multiple values (1,15), - defines a range (9-17), and / sets a step (*/5 means "every 5").

Tips to avoid common mistakes

  • Day-of-month vs. day-of-week: if you set both fields (positions 3 and 5) to non-* values, most cron implementations run the job when either matches, not both.
  • Time zone matters: a job at 0 9 * * * fires at 9 AM in the server's time zone, which may not be yours. Confirm the host's zone.
  • Steps need a base: */5 is fine, but ranges with steps like 0-30/10 only step within the stated range.
  • Validate before you ship: a single misplaced field can turn "once a day" into "every minute".
